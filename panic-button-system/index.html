Wireless and Wired Panic Button Technology
Wi-Fi Panic Buttons
How They Work:

Wi-Fi panic buttons connect directly to a facility's Wi-Fi network. When pressed, they send alerts to a central monitoring system or designated contacts via the network.

    Pros:
  • It has a broad range and functions anywhere within the building's Wi-Fi coverage.
  • No need for pairing with smartphones or additional BLE beacons.
  • Suitable for various industries due to reliable communication within Wi-Fi zones.
    • Cons:
  • Dependence on network stability and power.
  • Network outages or interference could delay or prevent alerts.
Cellular Panic Buttons
How They Work:

Cellular panic buttons connect directly to cellular networks, enabling them to send alerts to designated contacts or central monitoring systems without relying on Wi-Fi or paired devices.

    Pros:
  • Operate independently of local networks, providing greater flexibility and mobility.
  • Suitable for use in areas without Wi-Fi coverage or in outdoor environments.
    • Cons:
  • Dependent on cellular network availability, which may be limited in remote areas.
  • A cellular subscription is required, adding to operational costs.
Bluetooth Low Energy (BLE) Panic Buttons
How They Work:

BLE-based panic buttons use short-range Bluetooth signals to connect to smartphones or hubs. When pressed, they send a signal to the paired device, which triggers an alert to designated contacts or a central security system.

    Pros:
  • Energy-efficient, allowing long battery life for wearable devices.
  • Minimal hardware requirements by leveraging existing smartphone infrastructure.
    • Cons:
  • The limited range (up to 100 meters) may not suit large facilities.
  • Require a paired device (smartphone or hub) for alert transmission.
Radio Frequency Identification (RFID) Panic Buttons
How They Work:

RFID panic buttons use short-range radio frequencies to send signals to nearby RFID readers, often integrated with access control systems, which then trigger an alert.

    Pros:
  • Simple and effective for localized environments like small schools, businesses, or hospitals.
  • No need for complex network infrastructure.
    • Cons:
  • Limited to areas with RFID reader coverage.
  • Lack of flexibility for broader communication or mobile alerts.
Traditional Wired Panic Buttons
How They Work:

Traditional panic buttons are hardwired devices connected directly to a central security system. When pressed, they send an immediate alert through the wired connection to designated contacts or a monitoring center.

    Pros:
  • Highly reliable due to the direct, wired connection.
  • Not dependent on network or wireless signals, reducing risks of failure.
  • Suitable for environments requiring constant and uninterrupted security, like banks or retail stores.
    • Cons:
  • Limited mobility as they are fixed in place.
  • Installation can be complex and costly due to wiring requirements.
  • Lack of flexibility compared to wireless options
